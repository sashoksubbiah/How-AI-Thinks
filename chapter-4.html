Chapter 04

Backpropagation

The brilliant trick that makes training models with billions of weights possible — by working backwards.

In Chapter 3, we figured out how to adjust w₁ and w₂ by computing partial derivatives of the loss. We did it by writing out the full loss equation and manually differentiating. That worked fine for 2 weights. But when we have a model with a billion weights, can we really write out a billion equations and differentiate each one?

The Problem of Scale

To calculate the partial derivative of w₁ in Chapter 3, we treated w₂ as a constant and did a bunch of calculations — about as many as it took to compute the loss itself. Same for w₂. So with a billion weights, calculating all the partial derivatives naively would require a billion times as many calculations as computing the loss. Repeated for every single training step. That's infeasible.

🧩

The Maze Analogy: Imagine a maze with 100 entrances and one exit — a piece of cheese in the center. Starting from each entrance would be slow. But starting from the cheese and working backwards? You solve it once and get the path from every entrance. Backpropagation works the same way — by going backwards through the calculations, we compute all the partial derivatives in about the same time as one forward pass.

A Simple Example: f(x) = (x²)³

Forget turkeys for a moment. Let's take f(x) = (x²)³ and think of it as a machine with two steps:

Input: x
→
Step A: Square it (x²)
→
Step B: Cube it (input³)
→
Output

Forward Pass

Plug in x = 3:

Forward Pass
A
Input 3 → Square it → 3² = 9
B
Input 9 → Cube it → 9³ = 729
✓
So f(3) = 729

Backward Pass — The Key Idea

Now the question: if we increase x by a tiny amount, how much does the output change? We work backwards from the output:

Backward Pass
1
Start at the output. How much does a tiny change in the output affect... the output? Trivially: 1 (one times itself).
2
Step B backward. B does: input³. Derivative of x³ is 3x². At input = 9: 3 × 9² = 3 × 81 = 243.
Multiply by what came from the right: 1 × 243 = 243.
3
Step A backward. A does: input². Derivative of x² is 2x. At input = 3: 2 × 3 = 6.
Multiply by what came from B: 243 × 6 = 1,458.
✓
Result: If x increases by a tiny amount ε, the output increases by 1,458 × ε.

Let's verify: f(3) = 729, and f(3.01) = (3.01²)³ = (9.0601)³ = 743.68. The difference is 14.68, and 1458 × 0.01 = 14.58. Close! (The tiny discrepancy is because the derivative is exact only for infinitely small changes.)

Why Backward?

In this 2-step example, going forward or backward takes the same effort. But imagine 1000 steps in a chain, and you want the derivative at each one. Working backward lets you reuse the calculations you've already done — each step multiplies by one local derivative and passes it along. Working forward, you'd have to recompute from the output for each step independently.

It's like multiplying 32 × 93 × 38 and 14 × 93 × 38. If you start from the right, you compute 93 × 38 once and reuse it. Starting from the left, you compute it twice. With billions of weights, this reuse is the difference between feasible and impossible.

The Chain Rule

If this sounds familiar, it's because it's the chain rule from calculus. For a function f(g(x)), the derivative with respect to x is:

derivative of f w.r.t. x = (derivative of f w.r.t. g) × (derivative of g w.r.t. x)

With f(x) = x³ and g(x) = x², this gives us: 3(g(x))² × 2x = 3(x²)² × 2x = 3x⁴ × 2x = 6x⁵. At x = 3: 6 × 243 = 1,458. Same answer!

Backprop for the Turkey Model

Now let's apply this to our turkey feather model. The forward calculation flows left to right: take weights, multiply by turkey data, get predictions, compute errors, square them, add them up to get loss. The backward calculation flows right to left: starting from the loss, propagate the derivative back through each operation to figure out how each weight affects the loss.

Forward Pass (Review)

Forward: w₁=1000, w₂=3000
1
Predictions: T1=5500, T2=4500, T3=4250
2
Errors: T1=500, T2=1000, T3=−250
3
Squared errors: 250000, 1000000, 62500
4
Loss = 250000 + 1000000 + 62500 = 1,312,500

Backward Pass

Now we go backwards through each operation. For each step, we multiply the incoming gradient (from the right) by the local derivative. Here's the key: we need to figure out how a tiny change in w₁ (or w₂) would ripple through all the calculations and affect the final loss.

Backward Pass — Turkey #1's contribution to ∂loss/∂w₁
1
Start at loss. Gradient from right = 1
2
Sum backward. Sum just adds — derivative is 1 per input. Gradient stays 1.
3
Square backward. The square function: error². Derivative = 2 × error. Error for T1 = 500.
Gradient = 1 × 2 × 500 = 1,000
4
Subtract backward. (prediction − actual). Derivative w.r.t. prediction = 1.
Gradient = 1,000 × 1 = 1,000
5
Multiply backward. Prediction = height × w₁ + length × w₂. The partial derivative w.r.t. w₁ is height = 1.0.
Gradient = 1,000 × 1.0 = 1,000

That's Turkey #1's contribution. We do the same for Turkey #2 (contribution: 1,500) and Turkey #3 (contribution: −625). Adding them up: 1,000 + 1,500 + (−625) = 1,875. That matches our calculation from Chapter 3!

All three turkeys → ∂loss/∂w₁
T1
2 × 500 × 1.0 = 1,000
T2
2 × 1000 × 0.75 = 1,500
T3
2 × (−250) × 1.25 = −625
Σ
Total: 1,000 + 1,500 + (−625) = 1,875 ✓
All three turkeys → ∂loss/∂w₂
T1
2 × 500 × 1.5 = 1,500
T2
2 × 1000 × 1.25 = 2,500
T3
2 × (−250) × 1.0 = −500
Σ
Total: 1,500 + 2,500 + (−500) = 3,500 ✓

The Key Rules of Backpropagation

Rule 1: Multiply

Each step multiplies the incoming gradient by its local derivative. The local derivative depends on what operation the step performs (squaring, cubing, multiplying by a constant, etc.).

Rule 2: Add at Junctions

When multiple arrows come into a single node (like w₁ being used for all three turkeys), we add the gradients from each arrow. A small change in w₁ affects all three predictions, so its total gradient is the sum of its effects through each path.

Rule 3: Always Start with 1

The backward pass always starts with 1 at the output. "How much does a tiny change in the output affect the output?" — by definition, 1 times that tiny change.

The Big Picture: Training

You've now seen the full idea of how a training loop works:

① Start with random weights
↓
② Forward pass → compute predictions and loss
↓
③ Backward pass → compute gradient for every weight
↓
④ Update all weights: w = w − learning_rate × gradient
↓ repeat

This is what we did with 2 weights and 3 turkeys, and it's also what's used to train the models underlying ChatGPT with trillions of weights and tens of trillions of data points. The "turkeys" for language models are sequences of text — we'll see exactly what they look like starting in Chapter 8.

Key Insight

Backpropagation is the technique of propagating derivatives backwards through calculations to efficiently compute how each weight affects the loss. Without it, training models with millions or billions of parameters would be computationally impossible. With it, computing all the gradients takes about the same amount of work as one forward pass — regardless of how many weights there are.
