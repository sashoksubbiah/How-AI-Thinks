Chapter 07

Tokens

How AI reads text — not as letters or words, but as tokens: the fundamental unit of language for a GPT model.

You may have encountered tokens when paying for API access to models like ChatGPT — you pay a tiny fraction of a cent per token. But tokens aren't just a billing unit. They are a fundamental concept: GPT models are trained on tokens, take tokens as input, and generate tokens as output.

A tokenizer converts text to token IDs (encoding) and back (decoding). "He went to the store" becomes [3, 11, 10, 9, 8]. Since our model works with matrices of numbers (Chapter 5), we need this conversion. But real tokenizers are more sophisticated than simple word lists.

Bits, Bytes, and How Computers Store Text

Digital data is all 1s and 0s. Each 1 or 0 is a bit. A byte is 8 bits, giving 2⁸ = 256 possible values (0 through 255). Text is stored by mapping characters to bytes using encoding standards.

ASCII (1960s)

The first widely adopted standard. 'A' = 65, 'B' = 66, 'a' = 97, '!' = 33. Only 128 values — fine for English, hopeless for Tamil, Chinese, Arabic, or emoji.

UTF-8 (Modern Standard)

A clever encoding of Unicode that uses variable numbers of bytes per character. English letters: 1 byte. Tamil characters: 3 bytes. Emoji: 4 bytes. Nearly 300,000 characters supported.

Why Not Just Use Bytes as Tokens?

We could make each byte a token (256 tokens total). "He" would become two tokens: [72, 101]. This works, and the model could learn to predict byte sequences. But for a given number of weights, the model works much, much better if tokens are bigger units that carry meaning — like words.

🍓

The Strawberry Problem: Remember when early GPT models got "How many r's in strawberry?" wrong? Now you can see why. "Strawberry" might be a single token — the model never sees individual letters. To count r's, it needs to have learned how to spell a word it usually processes as one unit. Tokens have meaning; they are not letter-by-letter.

What we want: tokens that are word-like (carrying meaning), not too many (65,536 is a good target for a medium model), and lossless (can encode any text, even obscure characters, and decode it back perfectly).

Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

The elegant solution is a byte-level byte pair encoding tokenizer. The idea is simple: start with 256 tokens (one per byte), then repeatedly merge the most common adjacent pair into a new token until you reach your desired vocabulary size.

Worked Example: BPE with V = 261

Let's train a tiny tokenizer (5 merges beyond the base 256 byte tokens) on three sentences:

she runs. he also runs. she is running.

Start by encoding each word as UTF-8 bytes:

WordInitial Token IDs
she115, 104, 101
he104, 101
runs114, 117, 110, 115
also97, 108, 115, 111
running114, 117, 110, 110, 105, 110, 103
is105, 115

Now scan for the most frequent adjacent pair. The pair (104, 101) — "he" — appears in "she", "he", and we merge it into new token 256:

Real-World Tokenization

In practice, we train the tokenizer on billions of characters of text and merge until we reach a vocabulary of ~65,536 tokens. The most common words each become a single token. Less common words get split into pieces.

Common words like "the" are one token. Rare words get split into smaller pieces. Spaces are often included at the start of tokens.

Tamil Tokenization

Tamil characters take 3 bytes each in UTF-8, so Tamil words generally consume more tokens than English words of similar meaning. The BPE process naturally discovers common Tamil syllables and suffixes:

Special Tokens

Beyond regular text tokens, models reserve a few IDs for special purposes:

▶️

<bos>

Beginning of sequence. Signals the start of a new conversation or text.

⏹️

<eos>

End of sequence. Signals the model should stop generating.

🔧

<tool_call>

Signals the model wants to use an external tool (search, calculator, etc.).

👤

<user> / <assistant>

Mark whose turn it is in a conversation.

These special tokens are reserved from the vocabulary (e.g., the last 9 out of 65,536 IDs), so the total number of regular tokens is slightly less than V.

Why V = 65,536?

The vocabulary size V is a design choice. Too small → common words get split into too many pieces, wasting the model's processing power. Too large → too many rare tokens that never get properly trained, and the model's first and last layers (which are V-sized) become enormous.

V should be proportional to model size. A small model can't learn to predict among a million tokens. For a medium-sized model with 20–32 layers, V = 65,536 (which is 2¹⁶) works well. Larger models like GPT-4 may use 100,000+.

Key Insight

Tokens are the atoms of language for a GPT model. The BPE tokenizer elegantly discovers word-like units without any hand-crafted rules — it just counts frequent patterns. Common words become single tokens; rare words get split into meaningful pieces. The tokenizer is lossless: any text (English, Tamil, emoji, code) can be encoded and decoded perfectly. With our tokenizer in hand, we're ready to understand exactly what the model predicts.
