Chapter 22

RMS Normalization

Keeping numbers in a healthy range as they flow through 20+ layers — the automatic volume control for neural networks.

As numbers flow through many layers of multiplication and addition, they can grow wildly large or shrink to near-zero. This causes two problems: exploding gradients (numbers overflow) and vanishing gradients (numbers become too small for the computer to distinguish from zero). Normalization fixes this.

RMS Norm Step by Step

Normalizing the vector [3.0, 4.0, 0.0]
1
Square each element: [9.0, 16.0, 0.0]
2
Mean of squares: (9.0 + 16.0 + 0.0) / 3 = 8.333
3
Square root (= RMS): √8.333 = 2.887
4
Divide each element by RMS: [3.0/2.887, 4.0/2.887, 0.0/2.887] = [1.039, 1.386, 0.0]
5
Multiply by learned scale γ: Each dimension has its own learned scale factor. This lets the model adjust the magnitude per dimension if needed.

After normalization, the RMS of the vector is 1.0 — it's been rescaled to a standard magnitude. This happens before every attention and MLP sublayer in every transformer block. No matter how large or small the numbers entering a layer, normalization brings them to a consistent scale.

QK-Norm

Our model also normalizes the Query and Key vectors before computing attention scores. Without this, some Q-K dot products could be huge (causing softmax to concentrate on a single position) while others are tiny. QK-Norm ensures attention scores are well-behaved across all heads and layers.

Key Insight

RMS Normalization rescales vectors to unit RMS before each sublayer, preventing numbers from exploding or vanishing as they pass through 20+ layers. It's like an automatic volume control — without it, deep networks would be untrainable. The calculation is simple (square, mean, sqrt, divide) and adds negligible computation.
