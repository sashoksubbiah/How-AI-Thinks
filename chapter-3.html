Chapter 03

Training a Model: Predicting Feathers

Building a prediction machine, measuring how wrong it is, and using calculus to make it better — all with real numbers.

In Chapter 2 we talked about models for predicting words. Now we're going to build a model to predict something simpler: the number of feathers on a turkey. Don't ask — the point is to keep the numbers small enough that we can follow every calculation. You'll have to trust that these same ideas scale all the way up to predicting words in language models with billions of parameters.

The Setup

Think of our model as a machine. It takes two inputs — the height and length of a turkey — and gives one output: its prediction for the number of feathers.

The model's formula is simple: multiply the height by some number (call it w₁), multiply the length by another number (w₂), and add them together:

Prediction = height × w₁ + length × w₂

The numbers w₁ and w₂ are called weights — they're the knobs we can turn to make our model better or worse. Our data comes from three turkeys:

This data is made up for this example — not from real turkeys!

If our model is any good, then plugging in the heights and lengths should give predictions close to the actual feather counts.

Step 1: Start with Random Weights

Let's just pick some numbers: w₁ = 1000 and w₂ = 3000. Now calculate predictions for all three turkeys:

Forward Pass — Computing Predictions
1
Turkey #1: 1.00 × 1000 + 1.50 × 3000 = 1000 + 4500 = 5,500
2
Turkey #2: 0.75 × 1000 + 1.25 × 3000 = 750 + 3750 = 4,500
3
Turkey #3: 1.25 × 1000 + 1.00 × 3000 = 1250 + 3000 = 4,250

Not bad for random weights! Turkey #1 is predicted at 5,500 (actual: 5,000). But how do we turn "not bad" into a single number?

Step 2: Compute the Error and Loss

For each turkey, error = prediction − actual. Positive means we overshot, negative means we undershot:

Error Calculation
1
Turkey #1: 5,500 − 5,000 = +500 (overshot by 500)
2
Turkey #2: 4,500 − 3,500 = +1,000 (overshot by 1000)
3
Turkey #3: 4,250 − 4,500 = −250 (undershot by 250)

We want a single number that tells us how bad our overall prediction is. If we just add the errors, a big positive and a big negative could cancel out to zero even though both predictions are terrible. Solution: square each error first (which makes negatives positive and penalizes big errors more), then add them up. This sum is called the loss.

Loss Calculation
1
Squared error #1: 500² = 500 × 500 = 250,000
2
Squared error #2: 1000² = 1000 × 1000 = 1,000,000
3
Squared error #3: (−250)² = 250 × 250 = 62,500
Σ
Loss = 250,000 + 1,000,000 + 62,500 = 1,312,500
Why Loss Matters

Loss is a single number that says "how wrong is our model overall?" A loss of zero means perfect predictions. The entire goal of training is to make this number as small as possible. If we tweak our weights and the loss goes down — good. If it goes up — we moved in the wrong direction.

Step 3: Which Direction to Nudge?

We could guess new weights randomly, but it would be nice to know: if we increase w₁ a little bit, will the loss go up (bad) or down (good)? This is where derivatives from calculus come in.

🏔️

Analogy: Imagine you're blindfolded on a hillside and want to reach the lowest point. You can't see, but you can feel which direction the ground slopes beneath your feet. If it slopes down to the left, take a step left. The derivative tells us the slope — it tells us which direction is "downhill" for our loss.

Remember from high school: if f(x) = x², the derivative is 2x. At x = 3, the derivative is 6, meaning a tiny increase in x causes f(x) to increase by about 6 times that amount. We use the same idea for our loss — except we compute partial derivatives, one for each weight.

Partial Derivatives of Loss
w₁
∂loss/∂w₁ at (w₁=1000, w₂=3000) = 1,875
Positive → increasing w₁ increases loss → we should decrease w₁
w₂
∂loss/∂w₂ at (w₁=1000, w₂=3000) = 3,500
Positive → increasing w₂ increases loss → we should decrease w₂

Step 4: Update the Weights

We know which direction to move each weight. But by how much? Too little and we barely improve. Too much and we might overshoot. We use a learning rate (let's set it to 0.01) that controls the step size:

Weight Update
1
New w₁ = 1000 − (0.01 × 1,875) = 1000 − 18.75 = 981.25
2
New w₂ = 3000 − (0.01 × 3,500) = 3000 − 35.00 = 2,965
✓
New loss with these weights: 1,190,625 — down from 1,312,500!

It worked! The loss went down. Now we repeat — compute new partial derivatives at the new weights, update again, and keep going. This process of repeatedly computing gradients and nudging weights is called gradient descent.

After 1,000 Steps

After repeating this process 1,000 times, our weights converge to approximately w₁ ≈ 2,311 and w₂ ≈ 1,633, with a loss of about 133,190. Much better than the 1,312,500 we started with!

Notice w₁ goes down initially then back up — the gradient descent path isn't always a straight line to the optimum. It's like walking downhill on uneven terrain.

With these final weights, our predictions are: Turkey #1: 4,760 (actual 5,000, off by 240), Turkey #2: 3,775 (actual 3,500, off by 275), Turkey #3: 4,522 (actual 4,500, off by 22). Not perfect — but with only 2 weights and 3 data points, we can't expect perfection.

For the curious: the mathematically optimal weights are w₁ ≈ 2,347 and w₂ ≈ 1,604. We got close! A smaller learning rate and more steps would get us even closer.

The Training Loop

This is the core idea behind training every modern AI model:
① Start with random weights → ② Compute predictions (forward pass) → ③ Calculate loss → ④ Compute gradients (how much each weight affects the loss) → ⑤ Update weights using gradient × learning rate → ⑥ Repeat millions of times.

Our turkey model has 2 weights. ChatGPT has hundreds of billions. The process is identical — just way more weights, way more data, and way more steps.
