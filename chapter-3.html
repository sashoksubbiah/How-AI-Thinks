Chapter 3

Cricket, Chai & AI 🏏☕

How a computer learns from cricket matches to predict chai sales — one small mistake at a time.

Imagine you run a small chai stall just outside the MA Chidambaram Stadium in Chennai. On big match days you sell thousands of cups. On quieter matches, far fewer. Before each match begins, you want to predict exactly how many cups to prepare so nothing goes to waste.

The Big Idea

We build a prediction machine that takes two simple numbers about a match and predicts chai cups sold. Then we train it — it makes a guess, measures its mistake, and fixes itself. After hundreds of fixes it becomes surprisingly accurate.

🎯

Think of learning to bowl in cricket. Your first delivery goes wide. From that miss you adjust — move two steps left, change your wrist angle. Delivery after delivery you get closer to the stumps. AI learns the exact same way: guess → measure mistake → adjust → repeat.

The Formula

Building the Prediction Formula

We want a formula that uses our two inputs to predict chai cups. The simplest formula that makes sense:

Prediction = star_rating × w1 + crowd_hype × w2
w1
Weight for Stars
w2
Weight for Hype
🍛

Think of it like a biryani recipe. Rice and masala are the ingredients (star rating, crowd hype). The weights are like the recipe ratios — "for every unit of this, use THAT much." Finding the right weights = finding the right recipe. And just like cooking, you taste, adjust, taste again.

📖 The formula, broken down word by word
1
star_rating × w1 — Take the star rating (e.g. 8), multiply by Weight 1. This is the "stars contribution" to chai sales.
2
crowd_hype × w2 — Take crowd hype (e.g. 9), multiply by Weight 2. This is the "hype contribution."
3
Add them → That is our Prediction! Just multiply and add. That's it.
🧠 If w1 = 300 and w2 = 200, and a match has star_rating = 8 and crowd_hype = 9, what is the prediction?
✅ Sahi jawab! 8 × 300 = 2,400 (stars part) + 9 × 200 = 1,800 (hype part) = 4,200. The actual was 5,000 — so we're off by 800. Time to find better weights!
First Guess

Step 1 — Pick Random Weights 🎲

We have zero idea what w1 and w2 should be. So we just pick random numbers — say w1 = 300 and w2 = 250. Yes, they will be wrong. That's perfectly fine!

Why start random? Isn't that bad?

Think of a new chai shop owner on Day 1 — they don't know how much ginger is right. They make a batch, taste it, and adjust. We start random and fix from there. The clever part is how we fix it.

Measuring Mistakes

Step 2 — Measure the Error 📏

Error = Prediction − Actual. Predicted 4,650 but sold 5,000? Error = −350 (we guessed 350 too low).

🏏

Like a run-rate miss. Team needed 180 runs but scored 160 — error is −20 (fell short). Scored 200 — error is +20 (went over). Either way you need to know by how much you missed.

⚠️ The Cancelling Problem

Match 1 error: −350  |  Match 2 error: +50  |  Match 3 error: −900

If we just add: −350 + 50 − 900 = −1,200. But +1,000 and −1,000 would cancel to zero — suggesting perfect predictions when we're actually terrible! Adding raw errors is misleading.

The fix: square every error before adding. Squaring (multiply by itself) makes all numbers positive AND punishes big mistakes much harder than small ones.

📐 What does "squaring" mean?
?
Squaring = multiply the number by itself.
350² = 350 × 350 = 122,500
(−350)² = (−350) × (−350) = 122,500  ← negative disappears!
💡
Big mistakes get punished much more. An error of 100 → 10,000. But an error of 1,000 → 1,000,000. Ten times bigger error = hundred times bigger penalty. This pushes the model to avoid any huge single mistake.

Step 3 — The Loss Number 📉

Loss = sum of all squared errors. One number that says how good or bad the entire model is right now. Goal: drive it to zero (or as close as possible).

Loss = (error₁)² + (error₂)² + (error₃)²
✅ Loss = 0 → perfect predictions for every match
📈 Higher loss → our predictions are further from reality

Loss calculation with our first random weights

Match 1: Error = −350, Squared = 122,500
Match 2: Error = +50, Squared = 2,500
Match 3: Error = −900, Squared = 810,000
Loss = 122,500 + 2,500 + 810,000 = 935,000
🧠 After adjusting weights, Loss drops from 935,000 to 200,000. Is that good news?
✅ Bilkul sahi! Loss going down = predictions getting better. We always want loss as low as possible. Zero would mean perfect predictions every time!

Smart Direction

Step 4 — Which Way Do We Adjust? 🧭

Instead of blindly guessing new weights, math tells us exactly which direction to nudge each weight to reduce the loss. This uses a concept called a derivative — basically a slope detector.

🏔️

Imagine you are blindfolded somewhere in the Sahyadri mountains. Your goal is the lowest valley. You cannot see — but you can feel the ground tilt under your feet. If it slopes down to your left, step left. Always step downhill. This is Gradient Descent — walking down the "loss hill" to the lowest point.

🔢 The Derivative — a Slope Detector

The derivative tells us: "if I increase w1 a tiny bit, does the loss go up or down?" If the answer is "up" (positive slope), we should decrease w1. If "down" (negative slope), we should increase w1. It's just a signal pointing us in the right direction.

📐
Slope Explorer
Drag x along the curve. See the tangent line — it shows which way is downhill.
The curve f(x) = x² is shaped like a bowl. At any point, the slope of the tangent line tells you which way to step to reach the bottom (x = 0, where loss is lowest).
x = 4
At x = 4: f(x) = 16, slope = 8 → positive slope → decrease x to go downhill

In our chai model we have two weights. So we compute the slope for each one separately — these are called partial derivatives. One for w1, one for w2.

🔬 Partial derivatives — what they actually mean
∂w1
∂Loss/∂w1 = "if w1 increases by a tiny bit, how much does the loss change?" (while keeping w2 fixed)
Computed from our 3 match data points → gives us a number
∂w2
∂Loss/∂w2 = same idea but for w2 (while keeping w1 fixed)
A separate number telling us which way to move w2
📌
At our starting weights w1=300, w2=250:
∂Loss/∂w1 ≈ −16,100 → negative → increase w1 (it's too small!)
∂Loss/∂w2 ≈ −14,650 → negative → increase w2 (it's too small too!)
One Step

Step 5 — Update the Weights 🔧

We know the direction. Now we use a learning rate — a small number like 0.001 — to control how big our step is. Too big a step = overshoot the good answer. Too small = takes forever.

🍵

Like adjusting ginger in chai. If the batch is bland you don't dump in the whole jar. You add a pinch, taste, add another pinch. The learning rate is that pinch — small and careful, so you don't ruin the whole pot.

new_w1 = old_w1 − (learning_rate × slope_of_w1)
new_w2 = old_w2 − (learning_rate × slope_of_w2)
Learning rate: 0.001
Slope of w1 ≈ −16,100  → subtract a negative = increase w1
Slope of w2 ≈ −14,650  → subtract a negative = increase w2
new w1 = 300 − (0.001 × −16,100) = 300 + 16.1 = 316
new w2 = 250 − (0.001 × −14,650) = 250 + 14.65 = 265

Loss drops from 935,000 to around ~700,000. Better! Now repeat this hundreds of times.

The Big Picture

What You Just Learned 🎓

⚖️

Weights

Numbers the model learns. They control how much each input contributes to the prediction.

📉

Loss

Total squared error in one number. Always drive it down toward zero.

🏔️

Gradient Descent

Use the slope of loss to always step in the downhill direction.

🍵

Learning Rate

Step size. Too big = overshoot. Too small = too slow. Like a pinch of ginger.

🔁

Training Loop

Predict → measure loss → compute slope → update weights → repeat 1000s of times.

🧠

This IS AI!

ChatGPT, Gemini — every AI does this exact loop with billions of weights, not just two.

🎉 The Core Loop of AI Learning

1. Predict → 2. Measure loss → 3. Compute slope → 4. Nudge weights downhill → 5. Repeat → Accurate predictions — for chai, or for anything!
