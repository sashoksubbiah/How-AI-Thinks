Chapter 02

Predicting the Next Word

The simplest language model: counting word pairs and calculating probabilities.

Spell Checking: Where It All Started

Let's start with something familiar: spell checking. Is there a spelling error in this sentence?

He wnt to the store.

Yes. "Wnt" is a typo. A spell checker works by keeping a big list of valid words, breaking the sentence into words, and checking if each word is on the list. If "wnt" isn't found, it gets flagged. Pretty straightforward for English — you split on spaces and look things up.

But what about languages without spaces between words?

The Tamil Challenge

In Tamil, words can run together in complex ways, and the language is agglutinative — suffixes attach to root words to form long compound words. Consider:

அவன் கடைக்கு போனான்.

(He went to the store.)

Now imagine someone writes:

அவன் மலைக்கு போனான்.

(He went to the mountain.)

Is the second sentence wrong? Maybe, maybe not — it depends on context. But a computer could judge how likely it is by looking at statistics. How often does மலைக்கு (to the mountain) appear in text compared to கடைக்கு (to the store)? If the computer has seen கடைக்கு thousands of times and மலைக்கு very rarely, it might flag it as unusual.

The key idea: collect lots of text, count how often word combinations appear, and use those counts to judge if new text sounds right. This is exactly how a bigram model works — it counts pairs of consecutive words and calculates probabilities.

Building a Bigram Model

Let's work through a complete example. Imagine a strange land where the only words in their language are:

a, bed, he, house, is, man, red, store, the, to, went

We search for all the written text in this land and find these five sentences. This is our corpus — the body of text we'll learn from:

He is a man. He went to the store. He went to the house. He went to bed. The house is red.

Step 3: Calculate probabilities

Now the crucial step. "He" appears 4 times total. Out of those 4 times, it's followed by "went" 3 times and by "is" 1 time. So:

Calculating Conditional Probabilities
1
P("went" | "he") = count("he went") ÷ count("he") = 3 ÷ 4 = 75%
2
P("is" | "he") = count("he is") ÷ count("he") = 1 ÷ 4 = 25%
3
P("the" | "to") = count("to the") ÷ count("to") = 2 ÷ 3 = 67%
4
P("bed" | "to") = count("to bed") ÷ count("to") = 1 ÷ 3 = 33%

We can do this for every word pair and build a complete probability table:

The probability is calculated by dividing the pair count by the first word's total count.

Judging Sentences

Now we can use our model to judge if a sentence is likely or unlikely. Let's evaluate: "He went the store."

Judging a sentence
1
"He" → "went": probability = 75% ✓ Good
2
"went" → "the": probability = 0% ✗ Never seen!
3
"the" → "store": probability = 33%
!
Overall: 75% × 0% × 33% = 0% → This sentence is flagged as wrong!

The model caught that "went" should be followed by "to", not "the". The correct sentence "He went to the store" would score: 75% × 100% × 67% × 33% = 16.6% — not super high (this is a tiny corpus after all), but way more than 0%.

The Two Sides of the Coin

Here's a very important concept. Judging the probability of text and generating new text are two sides of the same coin.

To generate text, we can use the same probability table. Start with "he" and choose the most probable next word: "went" (75%). After "went" comes "to" (100%). After "to" comes "the" (67%). After "the" comes "house" (67%). We've generated: "He went to the house."

But if we always pick the most likely word, we'll generate the same sentence every time. What if instead of choosing the most likely word, we pick randomly according to the probability? With a 75% chance we pick "went" after "he", but 25% of the time we might get "is" instead, leading to "He is a man" or "He is red."

Key Terms

Vocabulary

The complete set of unique words (or tokens) the model knows. In our example: a, bed, he, house, is, man, red, store, the, to, went — 11 words. For a real model like ChatGPT, the vocabulary has tens of thousands of tokens.

Conditional Probability

The probability of something given that something else is true. Written as P(A | B) — read as "the probability of A given B." Example: P("went" | prior word is "he") = 75%. Without the condition, P("went") for a random word from all our text is only 3/22 ≈ 14%.

Probability Distribution

All the probabilities for every possibility, which must add up to 100%. For example, given the word "the", the probability distribution for the next word is: house=67%, store=33%, everything else=0%.

Corpus

The collection of text used to build the model. For our toy example it's five sentences. For real models, it's billions or trillions of words scraped from the internet, books, code, and more.

N-gram

A bigram is a 2-gram (pair of words). A trigram is a 3-gram (triple). N-gram models count sequences of N words and calculate probabilities. Our model is a bigram model.

N-grams in Your Pocket

N-grams are why you can type on your smartphone. Start typing and pay very close attention to the letters your fingers tap. Often they tap the wrong letter. But your phone is constantly calculating probabilities and correcting you. If you type "t" and then your finger taps between "g" and "h" on the touchscreen, perhaps a little closer to "g" — your phone says nope, the probability of "h" after "t" is so much higher that "g" must be wrong. The letter "h" gets typed and you don't even notice.

Predictive text is the same idea at the word level. Your phone watches what you type, looks in a table similar to the one we built, and suggests the three or four most likely next words. You tap the prediction instead of typing it out.

Tamil Predictive Text

This same technology is essential for Tamil typing. Tamil has a rich set of characters and complex agglutination (words built by adding suffixes). When you type in Tamil on a phone, the keyboard uses n-gram models to predict not just the next word but the next syllable or suffix.

Tamil Example

If you type அவன் கடை, the phone might predict:

க்கு (to — forming கடைக்கு, "to the store") — highest probability

யில் (at — forming கடையில், "at the store")

(store — keeping it as just கடை)

The model knows which suffixes are most likely after கடை because it counted millions of such patterns in Tamil text.

Limitations of Bigram Models

Bigram models are simple and useful, but limited. They can only look at the immediately previous word — they have no way to understand context from earlier in the sentence or paragraph. A bigram model can tell you that "He no" sounds wrong, but it can't take into account information from an earlier sentence, like a detective talking to a foreigner, where "He no come home" might be intentional dialogue.

The models we'll build in the coming chapters will be able to look at thousands of tokens of context — entire pages of text — when making their predictions. But the fundamental idea remains the same: predict the most likely next token given everything that came before.

Key Insight

Predicting the next word and generating text are two sides of the same coin. If you have a model that can predict what word likely comes next, you can use it to generate text by picking words one at a time according to those probabilities. This is the foundation of how ChatGPT and every other large language model works — they're next-token prediction machines, made enormously powerful by being able to consider vast amounts of context.
