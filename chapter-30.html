Being Precise About Precision

How computers store decimal numbers — and why using fewer bits for each number was a breakthrough for AI.

Throughout this guide we've talked about numbers — weights, gradients, embeddings, attention scores. But how does a computer actually store a number like 3.14159? And does it matter?

Floating Point Numbers

Computers store decimal numbers in floating point format. Each number gets a fixed number of bits split into three parts:

Anatomy of a Float
S
Sign bit (1 bit): Positive or negative
E
Exponent (several bits): How big or small the number is (like scientific notation's power of 10)
M
Mantissa/Fraction (remaining bits): The significant digits of the number

Three Precision Levels

FormatBitsExponentMantissaExample: πMemory per 1B params
FP32328 bits23 bits3.141592654 GB
BF16168 bits7 bits3.1406252 GB
FP884-5 bits2-3 bits3.01 GB

Why BF16 Is the Sweet Spot

BFloat16 — The AI Favourite

Same range as FP32 (8-bit exponent) — numbers can be as large or small as FP32. Only the precision is reduced (7 mantissa bits vs 23). For AI, this trade-off is perfect: the model doesn't need to know that π is 3.14159265 vs 3.140625. The coarse shape of the loss landscape is the same at either precision.

Practical impact: BF16 cuts memory in half and doubles compute speed. A model that would need 80 GB in FP32 fits in 40 GB in BF16 — fitting on a single GPU instead of two. Training that would take 10 days takes 5.

Mixed Precision Training

In practice, training uses mixed precision: most calculations happen in BF16 for speed, but a few critical operations (like accumulating gradients) use FP32 for accuracy. The weight updates are accumulated in FP32 and then cast back to BF16. This gives nearly all the speed benefits of BF16 with the accuracy of FP32.
